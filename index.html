Heat Waves: The Wrath of Climate Change?

Climate Change is the exisential threat to humanity. Unchecked, it is going to actually bake this planet. This is not hyperbole. It's real. And we have a moral obligation.
Joe Biden, President-Elect of the United States
Extreme heat weather has become more frequent in the United States in the recent decades. When people are exposed to extreme heat, they can suffer from potentially heat-related illnesses such as heat exhaustion and heat stroke. In the worst case, extreme weather can directly or indirectly increase the incidence of deaths. Specifically, extreme heat exposure contributes directly to deaths from cardiovascular and respiratory diseases. Further, extreme high air temperature is a contributing factor for alcohol poisoning and drug overdose deaths. It is also worth noting that prevailing hot conditions can trigger mental and behavioral disorders that lead to detrimental consequences. This website is designed to remember the lives lost due to natural heat and to alarm the public of the threat of extreme heat in the hope of calling for individual actions that stop climate change.

Despite these realities, little attention has been paid to the heat wave events prevailing across the country and the lives lost. Identifying patterns in which heat is most deadly to Americans can increase the publicâ€™s awareness of the danger posed by climate change on human health.

Across the country, heat caused at least 7,558 deaths between 1999 and 2010. Moreover, according to the CDC, this number has almost doubled over the last decade to 13757. Heat is thus the leading weather-related cause of deaths in the United States.

Seasonal and regional patterns in heat-related mortality are evident. Over 80% of heat-related mortality from 1999-2010 occurred during June to September when the average heat index is above 32 celsius. Moreover, taking population into account, states like Arizona and Nevada suffer the most from heat. This is likely due to the long summer time and levels of urbanizations in cities across these states. However, the precise reasons underlying the surprising amount of heat-related mortality in these places have not yet been documented. Projections indicate that the frequency, duration, and intensity of extreme heat will continue to increase as a consequence of climate change. This means the potential heat wave season will be longer, and extreme heat could catch communities off-guard if it happens earlier or later than expected in a particular area. This also means no escaping the heat even during nighttime. Unfortunately, heat waves are only mere indicators of a looming threat: climate change. With the increase in natural disasters such as wildfires and hurricanes, to the loss of wildlife and deforestation, we are at the edge of an irreversible climate apocalypse. The time to act is now.

Historical Natural Disasters (1930-2010)

(use mouse to hover over the states in the US Map)

Natural disasters (e.g. wildfires, hurricanes) have positive statisticial correlation with heat waves.

In this visualization, we display a map of the United States overlayed by colors representing the heat index of each state, with higher index values indicating hotter temperatures. The heat index for each state is captured by normalized temperature data gathered from regional weather stations. Through thousands of data points from various weather stations per state, this visualization displays each state's average heat index (relative to other states, and not indicating the actual temperature) ranging from 0 to 21, along this colorscale.

Relative to the average number of natural disasters per state (roughly 750), we observe that hotter states record historically higher number of natural disasters. We conclude that there is a positive correlation between historical temperatures and natural disasters.

Correlation between CO2 Emissions and Heat Waves (use the mouse to brush over timeline)


Climate Change. The biggest issue facing the 21st Century. In this visualization, we explore the correlation between the increase in carbon dioxide emissions (1981-2010) and the heat wave trends in North America.

Carbon dioxide emissions, the primary contributer to our modern climate crisis, have been steadily increasing within this past few decades. The increasing number of heat waves recorded annually in this 30-year timeframe indicate a positive correlation betweem the two phenomena. As the Earth warms due to the effects of climate change, heat waves increase in frequency and have disastrous consequences, as seen in the previous visualizations. In order to mitigate the effects of a warming Earth , we must reduce our carbon footprint and find solutions to prevent climate change.
